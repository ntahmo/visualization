## Assignment 2: Good and Bad Data Visualization

### Requirements:

-   Data visualizations are important tools for communication and convincing; we need to be able to evaluate the ways that data are presented in visual form to be critical consumers of information
-   To test your evaluation skills, locate two public data visualizations online, one good and one bad
    -   You can find data visualizations at <https://public.tableau.com/app/discover> or <https://datavizproject.com/>, or anywhere else you like!
-   For each visualization (good and bad):
    -   Explain (with reference to material covered up to date, along with readings and other scholarly sources, as needed) why you classified that visualization the way you did.

        ```         
        Following visualization best practice around qualities of data visualization (aesthetic, substantive, and perception) keeping into consideration context, audience, and data structure, I classied two example figures as follows:

        1. Good visualization: https://lindseypoulter.com/wdvp/ (left figure)
        ```

        ![](images/Screenshot 2026-01-26 190005-01.png)

        ```         
        Reason 1 (aesthetic): The visualizations is simple and not overwhelming. It uses consistent, muted colors, leaves enough white space, and uses one color to demonstrate the stark difference between high-income and all other countries.

        Reason 2 (substantive): Without evoking a biased emotion, the visualization makes a bold point. The author(s) reference the World Bank and use average values as comparisons, which they state.

        Reason 3 (perception): It is clear how different metrics vary across country categories. The intention of the author is clearly communicated as we can see the disparity on objective indicators.

        2. Bad visualization: https://public.tableau.com/app/profile/lani.he/viz/WomeninNobelPrice/Dashboard
        ```

        ![](images/Dashboard-01.png)

        ```         

        Reason 1 (aesthetic): The circular layout and decorative radial ticks add visual complexity without improving understanding, increasing cognitive load and distracting from the core message.

        Reason 2 (substantive): By contracting over 120 years showing the nomination pools, the visualization risks oversimplifying the causes of gender disparities from structural constraints within respective fields.

        Reason 3 (perception): The circular yet time-based visualization makes it difficult to accurately assess the frequency of women laureates over time and field of study.
        ```

    -   How could this data visualization have been improved?

        ```         
        Improvement 1 (aesthetic): Replace the circular layout with a linear timeline and faceted by study field to reduce clutte.

        Improvement 2 (substantive): Add context, such as the number of total awards or female nominations by field).

        Improvement 3 (perception): Use the same x-axis scale across fields to support accurate comparisons of female representation over time.
        ```
-   Word count should not exceed (as a maximum) 500 words for each visualization (i.e. 300 words for your good example and 500 for your bad example)
